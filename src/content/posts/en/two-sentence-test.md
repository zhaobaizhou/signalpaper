Here's a test I use before shipping anything: can I explain this decision in two sentences?

Not justify it. Not rationalize it. *Explain* it — meaning I can state what I'm doing and why it's right for this particular situation.

If I can't, I don't ship. Not yet.

---

The two-sentence constraint isn't arbitrary. It's calibrated to the shortest coherent explanation that includes both *what* and *why*.

One sentence forces omission. Three sentences allows hedging. Two is the minimum for honesty.

---

## What this looks like in practice

**Design decision**: "I removed the sidebar because the content didn't need persistent navigation — readers either scroll through or they don't."

**Writing cut**: "I cut the third paragraph because it was restating what the second paragraph already said. The cut makes the piece tighter."

**Feature decision**: "I'm not building dark mode first because zero of my current users have asked for it and it would delay the more important work by a week."

Each of those is two sentences. Each has a *what* and a *why*. None of them require hedging or vague appeals to intuition.

---

## Why intuition alone isn't enough

Intuition is real. Experienced practitioners develop taste that runs ahead of their articulation. That's legitimate.

But there's a failure mode where "intuition" becomes a cover for decisions you haven't examined. You made the choice quickly, it *felt* right, and now you're defending it without understanding it.

The two-sentence test catches that. If your explanation sounds like "I just know it's better this way," that's not taste — that's a gap.

You might still be right. But you should find out which, rather than shipping on the feeling.

---

## The secondary benefit

If you can explain a decision clearly, you can change it cleanly.

When a decision fails — and some will — the clean explanation tells you exactly what went wrong. You made the wrong call about X because you misjudged Y.

That's learnable. "It just felt off" isn't.
