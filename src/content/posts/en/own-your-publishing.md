For two years I published everything on Medium. Then I didn't. Here's what changed.

Medium's pitch is distribution. Someone might find your work there who wouldn't find it elsewhere. That's real, and I don't want to dismiss it. In the early days of building an audience, any distribution you can get is valuable.

But distribution is what platforms offer. They don't offer — and structurally can't offer — ownership.

## What ownership actually means

When I publish on my own site:

- The content doesn't disappear if the platform changes its policies
- The URL structure is mine to control
- The design reflects my priorities, not the platform's engagement metrics
- I can add pages and structures that have nothing to do with the post format
- I can change anything about the reading experience without asking permission

None of this is hypothetical. Medium has changed its layout, paywall behavior, and recommendation algorithm multiple times. Writers who built on those foundations had to adapt to each change. Some of those changes benefited them; many didn't.

## The platform is a tenant's market

When you publish exclusively on a platform, you're a tenant in a landlord's building. The building is nicer than anything you could afford on your own, and the location is better. But you can't paint the walls, you can't sublease, and the landlord can raise the rent or evict you.

The landlord in this case is the platform, and the "rent" is your engagement data, your audience attention, and your creative compliance with whatever the algorithm currently rewards.

## The practical arrangement

I don't think the choice is binary. The arrangement I've settled on:

**My site is the primary.** Everything I publish lives here first and permanently. The URL on my site is the canonical one.

**Platforms are distribution.** I cross-post to newsletters, syndicate to Medium (with canonical URL pointing back), share on social. These are funnels that bring readers to my site, not destinations in themselves.

This means my site gets the SEO benefit, the permanent URL, and the relationship with readers. Platforms get a copy that serves their purpose (discovery) without giving them my primary relationship with the content.

## What this costs

It's more work. You maintain a server, or a deployment pipeline, or learn enough web development to customize the site. You don't get the built-in distribution of a large platform.

You also don't get the social proof that comes from follower counts and prominent placement. Building your own readership from scratch is slower than riding platform virality.

## What you get

Control and longevity.

The post you publish today on your own site can be there in ten years, with the same URL, exactly as you wrote it. You can't say that about any platform.

For writing that's meant to last — analysis, essays, documentation of your thinking — that longevity is worth the cost.

---

For content that's meant to spread today and doesn't need to last, platforms are fine. Use them for that. But build your site as the place where the important work lives.
