There's an asymmetry in attention that takes a while to internalize.

What you pay attention to is, within limits, yours to control. You can choose what you read, whose work you follow, what problems you spend time on. These choices accumulate into something like a mental diet, and the quality of your thinking reflects that diet.

What pays attention to *you* is mostly not yours to control. You can't make people read your work. You can't make the algorithm surface it. You can't make your audience grow faster than it does.

---

The confusion happens when people try to optimize the second thing by optimizing for it directly, which usually means producing content shaped by what seems likely to get attention rather than what they actually have to say.

This is a kind of corruption. The content becomes about the audience rather than the thinking, and the thinking gets worse because it's not being done for its own sake anymore.

---

The better approach: focus entirely on the first thing.

Develop taste. Read the right things. Think carefully. Make things with genuine craft.

The second thing — what pays attention to you — tends to follow, eventually, from the first thing done well. Not quickly, and not reliably. But more reliably than chasing it directly.

---

This is not a guarantee of audience. Some excellent work is found late or not at all. But most enduring work was made by people who were paying attention to the work, not to the metrics.
