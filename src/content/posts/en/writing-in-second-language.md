I've been writing professionally in English for six years. It is not my first language.

This creates a specific set of challenges that don't exist for native speakers, and a specific set of advantages that they don't get either. This guide is for people in the same position.

## The cognitive task

Writing in your second language is not translating. Translation starts in the source language and converts. Real writing in a second language starts in the second language — at least in the beginning, before the sentence has fully formed.

When you realize you're translating instead of writing, the output shows it. The syntax is wrong. The idioms are false. The rhythm is off.

The goal is to get to a place where you're thinking directly in the second language, at least at the sentence level. This takes much longer than people think, and there's no shortcut.

## What actually helps

### 1. Read in the target language every day

Not textbooks. Not language-learning content. Actual prose, in the genre you want to write in. Read writers whose voice you want to study.

The goal isn't vocabulary acquisition. It's pattern absorption. Your brain is building a model of what sounds right. That model is what you draw on when you write.

I read at least one English essay every morning before writing. This primes the register and rhythm.

### 2. Write sentences, not ideas

The mistake most second-language writers make is forming the complete idea first, then trying to express it. The idea becomes fully formed in the native language, and then you translate it.

Instead, start typing. Let the sentence find itself in the target language. It will be worse at first. It will sound more like you eventually.

### 3. Find your specific vocabulary

Every writer has a set of words that are fully theirs — the particular nouns and verbs that appear with high frequency in their work because they're the exact words for what they're thinking.

Map your vocabulary. Know which English words you use with confidence and which ones you're guessing at. For the guesses, learn them properly. Look up the word you mean, not the word you used.

### 4. Ignore register until the draft is done

Register — the formality and social positioning of language — is the hardest thing to calibrate in a second language. You'll often be too formal or too casual without knowing it.

Don't try to fix register during drafting. Get the ideas down, then read the draft aloud and notice where the voice sounds wrong. Fix those spots specifically.

### 5. Get a native reader for the final pass

This isn't for content. It's for idioms and phrases that are technically correct but don't sound right to a native speaker. These are hard to find yourself, because if you knew they were wrong, you wouldn't have written them.

A native reader who can say "this sounds weird" is invaluable. You don't need an editor — you need a flag.

## The advantages

Writing in a second language forces precision. You can't hide behind idiomatic vagueness, because you don't have access to those idioms. You're forced to say what you mean directly.

This is actually an advantage in many writing contexts. Native-language writers often have *too much* access to familiar phrasings — they reach for them automatically and sometimes obscure what they actually mean.

The second-language writer is, in some ways, writing more carefully.

---

## The honest prognosis

Your writing will never be quite like a native speaker's. It will have a slightly different texture. This is not a flaw. Most readers can't identify it, and many who can find it interesting.

What it won't be: invisible. And that's fine. The goal isn't to sound like everyone else. The goal is to say something worth reading in a way that works.

You can do that from your second language.
