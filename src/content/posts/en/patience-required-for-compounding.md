The curve of compounding looks flat at the start.

You write for six months and your audience doesn't grow. You build in public for a year and nobody notices. You practice a skill obsessively and the results look the same as they did on day one.

This is not evidence that compounding isn't working. It's the necessary shape of compounding.

---

Compounding's power comes from the later phases, where growth is exponential relative to the base. But those later phases are only available to people who survive the early flat phase, which looks — correctly — like nothing is happening.

The flat phase *is* something happening. You're building the base. You're installing the habit. You're developing the judgment about what's worth doing that you can't get from just thinking about it.

But it doesn't *look* like anything is happening, so most people quit.

---

## The measurement problem

Part of the problem is that we measure the wrong things.

We measure output: posts written, code shipped, revenue earned. And in the early phase, those numbers are small.

What we're not measuring: how quickly judgment improves, how much the quality floor has risen, how much faster iteration has become. These compound too, but invisibly, and they're the foundation for the output gains that will eventually be visible.

---

## A personal example

In the first year of writing publicly, I published sporadically. Maybe twenty pieces. The audience was small. The quality was uneven.

But I had installed the habit and built the base rate. By year two, I could write faster and think more clearly on the page. By year three, the pieces were finding audiences.

The three-year output curve looks impressive. But it was funded by two years that didn't.

---

## What to measure in the early phase

- Are you maintaining the habit?
- Is the work getting slightly less embarrassing over time?
- Are you still interested?

If the answer to those three is yes, compounding is working. Not visibly. But working.

The patience isn't passive. It's active commitment to staying in the game long enough for the math to show up.
