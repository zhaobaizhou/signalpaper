Technical debt is a well-understood concept: when you write code quickly at the expense of quality, you accrue a debt that slows future progress. The debt is invisible until it isn't.

There's an equivalent concept I think about for personal and organizational decision-making: *decision debt*.

Decision debt accrues when you delay or avoid decisions that you know need to be made. The un-made decision doesn't disappear. It stays in a mental queue, consuming background attention, and its presence shapes future decisions in ways you often can't trace.

---

## How it accumulates

Most decision debt comes from decisions that feel premature. "I don't have enough information yet." "Let's see how the next quarter goes." "This isn't urgent."

Sometimes that's correct. Some decisions genuinely need more data. But the pattern becomes a habit, and the habit turns into a backlog of decisions that are now both urgent and difficult, because they've been delayed so long that the cost of making them has risen.

A small example: whether to cut a product feature that users don't use. The decision feels difficult, so you delay it. The feature accumulates maintenance cost and takes up documentation space. New features are designed around it. A year later, cutting it is much harder than it would have been at month three.

---

## The background attention tax

The insidious part of decision debt is the attention it consumes when you're not actively thinking about it.

Research on rumination suggests that incomplete tasks generate involuntary intrusive thoughts — you're in the middle of something else and the un-made decision surfaces. This is the Zeigarnik effect. Incomplete items demand more attention than completed ones.

Every un-made decision in your queue is paying this tax, every day. The tax compounds.

---

## How to reduce it

**Schedule decision-making time.** Put it on the calendar the same way you'd put a deliverable. Un-made decisions don't resolve themselves through passive waiting; they need active time.

**Lower the bar for "good enough."** Most decisions don't require perfect information. An 80% right decision made now is often better than a 90% right decision made after four more weeks of deliberation. The difference in quality rarely matters as much as the difference in timing.

**Write decisions down when you make them.** This closes the mental loop. The Zeigarnik effect requires *incompleteness*. A written decision — even a provisional one — signals completion to your brain and stops the background processing.

**Make provisional decisions explicit.** If you genuinely need to wait, write down "I am waiting for X before deciding Y, and I will revisit this on [date]." This is different from avoidance. The timeline is set, and the decision-in-waiting doesn't accumulate the same background attention.

---

There's a kind of productivity that looks like speed but is actually just keeping the decision queue empty. The work itself doesn't have to be fast. The decisions that enable the work need to be made.
