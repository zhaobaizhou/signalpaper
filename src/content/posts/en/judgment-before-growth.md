A personal site has one real job: to show how you think.

Not how productive you are. Not how many things you've shipped. Not how many followers you have.

The trap most people fall into is treating a personal site like a portfolio—a container for accomplishments. But accomplishments are backward-looking. They prove you did something. They don't explain how you'd approach the next thing.

## The signal problem

When someone lands on your site, they're trying to answer a question: *Should I trust this person's judgment?*

That question can't be answered by a list of projects. It can only be answered by reading how you think. Do you see clearly? Do you get to the point? Do you notice things others miss?

**Volume is easier to fake than taste.**

A site with ten thoughtful, honest essays is worth more than a site with a hundred posts optimized for traffic. One well-argued position reveals more about your mind than a dozen summaries of other people's ideas.

## What judgment looks like

Judgment shows up in small things:

- What you choose to write about (not everything deserves a post)
- What you leave out (the edit is the argument)
- How you handle uncertainty (do you fake confidence, or name what you don't know?)
- Whether your descriptions match reality (do you call things what they are?)

You can't fake these over time. The site either reveals a mind worth knowing, or it doesn't.

## The practical implication

Before adding a new section to your site, ask: *Does this show how I think, or does it just show what I've done?*

If it's the latter, it might belong in a résumé instead.
