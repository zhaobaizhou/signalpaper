Before a reader reads the first word, they've already made a judgment about the piece.

That judgment comes from typography. Font size, line height, paragraph spacing, margin width. These elements communicate before the semantic content is processed.

The signal is: **does the author care about the reading experience?**

---

## Dense text communicates arrogance

Paragraphs without adequate breathing room, lines stacked tightly, force the reader to do extra work to separate and track lines. That work is real cognitive load.

When you don't give your text breathing room, you're saying: the content is mine, your comfort is secondary. Readers feel this even if they can't articulate why.

---

## Font size communicates respect

At 16px, body text is functional. At 18px, it's noticeably more comfortable. The difference looks small, but after extended reading, the effect is significant.

This isn't about minimum readability thresholds. It's about telling the reader: your eyes are worth the extra 2 pixels.

## Line height communicates rhythm

For body text, 1.6 is functional, 1.75 is comfortable, 1.8 is generous. Below 1.6, eyes struggle to jump accurately from line end back to line start.

Rhythm isn't only for music. Reading has rhythm, and good typography supports it.

---

## Typography attitude in practice

When I review a design, I don't ask "does this look beautiful?" I ask "what does this tell the reader?"

Tight line spacing and small text tells the reader: it's hard to read here. Generous spacing and appropriate size tells the reader: this is made for you.

That's attitude, expressed in pixels.

---

## The standards

Here's what I think is worth aiming for:

| Property | Target |
|:---|:---|
| Body font size (desktop) | 17–18px |
| Body font size (mobile) | 16px |
| Line height | 1.75–1.85 |
| Paragraph spacing | ~1.5em |
| Content width | 680–780px |
| Font | System stack or quality web font |

These aren't rules. They're defaults in the absence of strong reason to deviate.

---

Good typography is polite. It says: I thought about how you'd read this. That's already more than most web pages do.
