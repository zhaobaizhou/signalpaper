There's a category of knowledge that can't be written down fully. Not because it's secret, but because it's *procedural* — it lives in the doing, not in the description of doing.

Michael Polanyi called it tacit knowledge. The classic example: you can't explain how to ride a bicycle well enough that someone reads the explanation and can then ride. The knowledge transfers through practice, not description.

This matters for knowledge workers, because almost everything interesting we do involves significant tacit components that our written systems can't capture.

## What tacit knowledge includes

- Knowing when code is "ready" versus when it needs one more pass
- Calibrating how much context a piece of writing needs to be understood by a first-time reader
- Feeling when a product decision is premature
- Recognizing a good lead paragraph from a mediocre one

These look like intuitions. They are — but they're *trained* intuitions, built through many repetitions with feedback. The knowledge is real. It just doesn't live in your notes.

## The documentation trap

A common failure mode: someone gets good at something, documents their process exhaustively, and expects the documentation to transfer the skill.

It doesn't. Or rather, it transfers the *explicit* part of the skill while leaving the tacit part behind. Someone following the documentation will produce technically compliant output that's missing the qualities that made the original good.

The documentation is useful. It's not sufficient.

## What actually transfers tacit knowledge

**Apprenticeship**: Watching an expert work and having them react in real time. "That's good, keep going" / "wait, that's off" — those micro-corrections install the tacit knowledge that no document can convey.

**Deliberate practice with feedback**: Not just doing the thing, but doing it with attention to quality and with external feedback from someone whose judgment you trust. The feedback is the mechanism for installing the knowledge.

**Volume with reflection**: Doing something many times and having a practice of reviewing what worked and what didn't. The reflection is what converts raw experience into transferable insight.

## Personal note

I've noticed that the skills I'm most confident in are ones I've done with a lot of feedback, not ones I've read about extensively. Reading contributes — it accelerates the feedback loops and gives vocabulary for what's happening — but the knowledge doesn't settle until the practice does.

The practical implication: when I want to get better at something, I look for feedback loops first. Documentation and reading are inputs to those loops, not replacements for them.

This also explains why expertise is hard to scale. You can document everything and still not produce experts. The tacit part requires the doing.
